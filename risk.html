Risk Summary

Estimated reading time: 2 min

Due to the potential for losses, the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) considers this investment to be high risk.

What are the key risks?

1. You could lose all the money you invest

If the business you invest in fails, you are likely to lose 100% of the money you invested. Most start-up businesses fail.

2. You are unlikely to be protected if something goes wrong

Protection from the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS), which usually covers certain investments up to a limit, does not normally cover this type of investment. You are unlikely to be able to claim from the FSCS if something goes wrong. Learn more about FSCS protection at fscs.org.uk.

Protection from the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) does not cover poor investment performance. If you have a complaint against an FCA-regulated firm, the FOS may be able to consider it. Learn more about FOS protection at financial-ombudsman.org.uk.

3. You won't get your money back quickly

Even if the business you invest in is successful, it will likely take several years to get your money back. You are unlikely to be able to sell your investment early. The most likely way to get your money back is if the business is bought by another business or lists its shares on an exchange such as the London Stock Exchange. These events are not common.

If you are investing in a start-up or an early-stage business, you should not expect to get your money back through dividends. Start-ups rarely pay these.

4. Don't put all your eggs in one basket

Putting all your money into a single business or type of investment is risky. Spreading your money across different investments makes you less dependent on any one to do well. A good rule of thumb is not to invest more than 10% of your money in high-risk investments. Learn more at the FCA's InvestSmart.

5. The value of your investment can be reduced

The percentage of the business that you own will decrease if the business issues more shares. This could mean that the value of your investment reduces, depending on how much the business grows. Most start-up businesses issue several rounds of shares. These new shares could have additional rights that your shares don't have, such as the right to receive a fixed dividend, which could further reduce your chances of getting a return on your investment.

How this applies to DoubleCheque

The DoubleCheque fund invests in early-stage UK companies through SEIS and EIS. These are exactly the kind of high-risk, illiquid, unlisted investments described above. Tax reliefs depend on your individual circumstances and on the companies maintaining their qualifying status, and the rules may change. The fund is an unapproved Alternative Investment Fund and is not an HMRC-approved fund. You should read the Information Memorandum and Key Information Document in full, and take your own professional advice, before investing.

If you are interested in learning more about how to protect yourself, visit the FCA's website at fca.org.uk/investsmart.
